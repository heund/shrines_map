# Spatial Analysis

## Analytical Aim

Spatial analysis in this project is intended to identify locations where overlapping layers create meaningful conditions for QR interaction. The objective is not simply to measure nearest distance or density in a generic GIS sense. The analysis must determine how heritage designation, biennale presence, and shrine significance co-produce different kinds of spatial relevance.

The three layers are analytically comparable because they occupy the same city, but they are not conceptually identical. Each layer expresses a different mode of presence: institutional heritage recognition, contemporary curatorial activation, and ritual or locally embedded significance. Spatial comparison should therefore attend to relation, not only co-location.

## Comparative Layer Logic

The following comparisons structure the analysis.

### Heritage Sites and Venues

This comparison identifies where formal cultural recognition and current biennale infrastructure come into proximity. Such overlap may indicate areas where historical legibility and contemporary programming reinforce one another, making them plausible sites for intensified public engagement.

### Venues and Shrines

This comparison examines where curatorial circulation intersects with ritual or vernacular spatial memory. These zones may be especially important for QR placement because they can carry both programmed visitation and latent local significance, even when shrine data remains uncertain or access-limited.

### Heritage Sites and Shrines

This comparison highlights where formal recognition and less formalized sacred or inherited place-making exist near one another. The relation may reveal alignment, omission, or tension between institutional cultural frameworks and localized spatial knowledge.

### Three-Layer Overlap

Where all three layers converge, the system should treat the location as potentially high-value for relational analysis. Such convergence does not automatically imply suitability for public QR placement, but it does indicate a dense field of significance that may warrant closer review.

## Relational Density

Relational density refers to the degree to which a location is situated within meaningful cross-layer relations. It is not identical to simple point clustering. A location may have high relational density when:

- multiple layer types occur within analytically relevant proximity
- the surrounding points represent different regimes of significance rather than duplicates of the same type
- access conditions allow at least some form of situated engagement
- uncertainty is documented well enough to support cautious interpretation

Relational density should therefore be understood as a qualitative-structural measure supported by spatial evidence. It identifies areas where interaction is likely to resonate across multiple cultural and operational frames.

## Interaction Potential

Interaction potential describes the likelihood that a QR point can generate meaningful engagement rather than incidental scans. It should be assessed through a combination of:

- relational density across layers
- accessibility of the immediate site
- visibility policy and ethical constraints
- likelihood of visitor passage through the area
- contextual suitability for repeated encounter

A location with lower geometric centrality may still have high interaction potential if it sits at a strong narrative junction between layers. Conversely, a geometrically convenient site may have limited interaction potential if it lacks relational significance or presents ethical constraints.

## Dwell-Time Influence

Dwell time matters because it distinguishes passing contact from sustained attention. In this system, dwell time should be treated as a spatially meaningful variable, not just a behavioral metric.

Longer dwell time may indicate:

- greater interpretive engagement at the site
- a location conducive to reflection or repeated observation
- stronger conditions for narrative transformation input
- more durable linkage between site and participant behavior

When dwell time accumulates at a location already characterized by high relational density, the site may warrant greater analytical weight within the broader system. This does not prescribe a fixed formula, but it does establish that time spent in place changes the significance of interaction.

## Revisit Patterns and Spatial Recurrence

Revisit patterns indicate whether a site functions as a one-time encounter or a recurring node within participant movement. Repeated return to a location may suggest durable attachment, route dependence, ritual repetition, or unfinished engagement. These patterns become especially meaningful when revisits occur across sites with different layer compositions.

Spatial recurrence should therefore be examined both at the single-site level and across related clusters. A participant who repeatedly returns to a venue near a shrine cluster may be generating a different kind of signal from a participant who visits multiple isolated venue sites once each.

## Analytical Cautions

- High overlap does not automatically justify public-facing placement.
- Approximate shrine locations should not be overinterpreted as exact relational anchors.
- Dense public circulation does not necessarily imply ethical suitability.
- Areas with undefined shrine coordinates may still matter conceptually and should not be treated as absent.

## Output Orientation

Spatial analysis should produce a ranked and interpretable understanding of where QR interaction may be most meaningful, most sensitive, or most transformative. The resulting outputs should support research decisions about placement, visibility, and later narrative modulation without reducing the city to a uniform field of equivalent points.
