# Project Overview

## Purpose

This project defines a research-driven spatial mapping system for Jeju City, South Korea. Its purpose is not merely to display points on a map, but to identify locations where QR-based interaction can carry differentiated narrative and relational meaning. Spatial placement is treated as an active condition within a larger interpretive system: where a QR point is placed affects what kinds of interaction occur there, what data can be gathered, and how those interactions contribute to later transformation processes.

The mapping system supports a broader dynamic framework in which visitor behavior, especially QR scans, dwell time, and revisit patterns, accumulates as situated evidence. That evidence does not remain neutral. It informs changing narrative states and modulates the intensity of transformation across the system. In this sense, the map is not a passive substrate; it is part of the logic through which interaction becomes legible and consequential.

## Spatial Layers

The project is organized around three primary spatial layers.

### 1. Heritage Sites

Heritage sites are sourced from the Korea Heritage Service and represent formally recognized cultural locations. This layer contributes institutional and historical reference points, including site name, geographic location, and classification type. It establishes a framework of officially legible cultural significance within Jeju City.

### 2. Biennale Venues

Biennale venues represent the active exhibition and event infrastructure of the Jeju Biennale. This layer identifies the contemporary curatorial footprint of the project through venue names, coordinates, and reference map links. It anchors the system in present-day movement patterns and programmed public access.

### 3. Shrines

The shrine layer records sites of ritual, vernacular, or locally transmitted significance. Unlike the heritage layer, shrine data contains ambiguity, uneven documentation, and variable ownership conditions. These are not treated as defects to be erased. They are integral to the research value of the dataset because they reveal uneven visibility, contested legibility, and differing levels of spatial certainty.

## Why QR Placement Matters

QR interaction points are positioned in relation to these overlapping layers rather than in isolation. A location gains analytical importance when it sits within a meaningful relation to heritage designation, biennale activity, shrine presence, or some combination of the three. The resulting map is therefore a relational instrument: it identifies not only where interaction can happen, but where interaction is likely to matter differently.

QR scans provide event-level evidence of encounter. Dwell time suggests the depth or duration of engagement at a site. Revisit patterns indicate recurrence, attachment, or continued circulation through a location. These signals help distinguish transient contact from sustained involvement, allowing the system to infer varying intensities of site activation.

## Dynamic Transformation

The wider system uses spatially grounded interaction data to influence changing narrative representations, including what the project describes as "deity expressions." This documentation does not specify internal transformation mechanics in detail; however, it establishes the governing principle that transformation is both cumulative and spatially conditioned.

Not every interaction should carry identical weight. Interactions occurring at sites with stronger relational density, clearer access status, or repeated visitation may contribute differently from interactions at peripheral or uncertain locations. The project therefore requires a mapping framework that preserves provenance, uncertainty, and access sensitivity so that later transformation logic remains accountable to the conditions under which the data was produced.

## Research Orientation

This project should be understood as a research system with interpretive and ethical obligations. Its outputs must remain sensitive to differences in ownership, accessibility, confidence of geolocation, and cultural exposure. The map is not intended to flatten all locations into equivalent coordinates. Instead, it should preserve distinctions between official heritage sites, curatorial venues, and shrines whose visibility may be partial, uncertain, or socially sensitive.
